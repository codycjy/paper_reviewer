# Review

## Summary
This paper introduces GNNXEMPLAR, a novel global explanation method for GNNs inspired by exemplar theory from cognitive science. GNNXEMPLAR identifies representative nodes in the GNN embedding space, called exemplars, and uses natural language rules to explain predictions based on the characteristics of these exemplars' neighborhoods. The method involves selecting exemplars through a coverage maximization problem using reverse k-nearest neighbors and using a self-refining prompt strategy with large language models (LLMs) to derive interpretable rules. Experiments show that GNNXEMPLAR outperforms existing methods in fidelity, scalability, and human interpretability, as validated by a user study.

## Strengths
1. GNNXEMPLAR introduces a novel approach to GNN explainability by leveraging exemplar theory, which is a unique perspective in the field of interpretability.
2. The paper addresses the limitations of existing global GNN explainers, particularly in large, real-world graphs with complex node attributes and structure-attribute interactions.
3. The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation of GNNXEMPLAR, including experiments on diverse benchmarks and a user study with 60 participants, demonstrating the method's effectiveness and scalability.

## Weaknesses
1. GNNXEMPLAR requires access to the GNN's embedding space, which may not be available for all GNN architectures, limiting its applicability.
2. The method involves multiple steps, including exemplar selection and self-refining prompt strategy, which might be computationally intensive and challenging to implement.
3. The paper does not thoroughly discuss the robustness of GNNXEMPLAR to potential changes in the GNN architecture or input graph structure.

## Questions
1. How does GNNXEMPLAR handle GNNs that produce continuous output scores rather than discrete class labels?
2. What are the computational requirements for implementing GNNXEMPLAR on large graphs with millions of nodes?
3. How does the method ensure that the natural language rules generated by LLMs are faithful to the GNN's decision-making process?

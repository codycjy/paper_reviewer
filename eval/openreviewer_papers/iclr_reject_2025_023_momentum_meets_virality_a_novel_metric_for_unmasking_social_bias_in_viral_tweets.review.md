# Review

## Summary
This paper presents a new metric, the ViralTweet Score (VTS), designed to predict the virality of tweets by accounting for social biases. The authors also release the ViralTweets Dataset with 88.8k labeled tweets in Hindi. The study aims to address the limitations of existing metrics by introducing a physics-inspired momentum-based approach. The paper evaluates the effectiveness of VTS using two methodologies and compares its performance against other metrics.

## Strengths
1. Novel Metric: The introduction of the ViralTweet Score (VTS) represents a novel contribution to the field of computational social science. By incorporating social biases, VTS offers a more comprehensive approach to predicting tweet virality compared to existing metrics.
2. Dataset Release: The release of the ViralTweets Dataset with 88.8k labeled tweets in Hindi is a valuable resource for researchers, particularly in the under-resourced Indic ecosystem. The dataset provides detailed engagement metrics and social bias labels, enhancing the reproducibility of future studies.
3. Focus on Social Bias: The paper addresses an important aspect of tweet virality by examining the relationship between social biases and virality. This focus is crucial for understanding how biased content spreads and the implications for broader public discourse.

## Weaknesses
1. Lack of Theoretical Foundation: The paper does not provide a strong theoretical basis for why the VTS metric is effective in capturing virality. A more rigorous theoretical framework would strengthen the claims made about the metric's performance.
2. Limited Comparative Analysis: The comparison with existing metrics is not exhaustive. The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison to highlight the advantages and limitations of VTS relative to other state-of-the-art methods.
3. Scope Limitation: The study focuses specifically on Hindi tweets and the Indian context. While this provides insights into social media dynamics in one cultural setting, it may limit the generalizability of the findings to other languages and social contexts.
4. Data Collection Period: The tweets were collected between January 2019 and October 2019, which may not reflect current social media trends or behaviors. Social media platforms and user interactions evolve rapidly, and older data may not capture the current dynamics.
5. Reliance on LLMs for Bias Labeling: The use of large language models (LLMs) for automatically labeling social biases may introduce its own set of biases or inaccuracies. The paper does not thoroughly discuss the potential limitations of this approach or the reliability of the bias labels.

## Questions
1. Theoretical Justification: Can you provide a theoretical explanation for why the VTS metric is effective in capturing tweet virality, especially considering the influence of social biases?
2. Comparative Performance: How does the VTS metric compare to other existing metrics in terms of accuracy and effectiveness in predicting tweet virality?
3. Generalizability: Do you anticipate that the findings from this study will generalize to other languages and social contexts beyond Hindi tweets in India?
4. Data Collection Period: How do you think the results might differ if the data were collected in the present day, given the rapid evolution of social media platforms and user behaviors?
5. Bias Labeling: What measures were taken to ensure the reliability and accuracy of the social bias labels generated by LLMs? How do you address potential biases introduced by these models?

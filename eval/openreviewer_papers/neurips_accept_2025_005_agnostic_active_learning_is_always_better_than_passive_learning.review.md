# Review

## Summary
This paper studies agnostic active learning and passive learning. It shows that the sample complexity of agnostic active learning is always smaller than that of passive learning by a factor proportional to the best-in-class error rate. This closes a qualitative and quantitative gap in our understanding of active learning in the non-realizable case. The main technique is to show that the optimal first-order query complexity of agnostic active learning admits a leading term which is always strictly smaller than the sample complexity of passive supervised learning.

## Strengths
- This paper resolves a long-standing open question of central importance to the theory of active learning. It shows that the sample complexity of agnostic active learning is always smaller than that of passive learning by a factor proportional to the best-in-class error rate. This closes a qualitative and quantitative gap in our understanding of active learning in the non-realizable case. 
- The main technique is to show that the optimal first-order query complexity of agnostic active learning admits a leading term which is always strictly smaller than the sample complexity of passive supervised learning. This is a very nice idea and I think it has further applications in the future.

## Weaknesses
- The main result of this paper shows that the sample complexity of agnostic active learning is always smaller than that of passive learning by a factor proportional to the best-in-class error rate. However, the result does not show the exact difference between the sample complexity of agnostic active learning and that of passive learning. In other words, the result does not show how much active learning can improve the sample complexity of passive learning. It would be better if the authors could provide a lower bound on the difference between the sample complexity of agnostic active learning and that of passive learning.

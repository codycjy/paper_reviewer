# Review

## Summary
This paper derives a PAC-Bayesian generalization bound for RL that explicitly accounts for temporal dependencies arising from Markovian data. The authors integrate a bounded-differences condition on the negative empirical return to establish the applicability of a McDiarmid-style concentration inequality tailored to dependent sequences such as Markov Decision Processes. This leads to a PAC-Bayes bound with explicit dependence on the Markov chain’s mixing time. The authors show that the bound can be directly applied to off-policy RL algorithms in continuous control settings, such as Soft Actor-Critic. Empirically, they demonstrate that the bound yields meaningful confidence certificates for treatment policies in simulated healthcare environments, providing high-probability guarantees on policy performance.

## Strengths
The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a comprehensive overview of the problem and the existing literature, and they clearly state their contributions. The theoretical results appear to be sound, and the experimental results support the authors' claims. The paper addresses an important problem in RL, and the proposed solution has the potential to improve the safety and reliability of RL policies used in healthcare and other high-stakes domains.

## Weaknesses
1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods. While the authors compare their approach to standard RL algorithms, they do not compare it to other state-of-the-art methods in the field of safe RL or RL for healthcare. This makes it difficult to assess the true novelty and effectiveness of the proposed approach.
2. The experimental evaluation is limited to a small set of environments. The authors evaluate their approach on three environments: Sepsis, HalfCheetah, and Humanoid. While these environments represent different types of RL problems, they are still relatively simple compared to some of the more challenging environments found in real-world healthcare settings. For example, the authors could evaluate their approach on more complex environments such as the ICU management environment or the virtual patient environment.
3. The paper could benefit from a more thorough analysis of the results. While the authors provide some high-level insights into the performance of their approach, they do not provide a more detailed analysis of the results. For example, they could analyze the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance of the approach or provide a more detailed analysis of the confidence certificates generated by their approach.

## Questions
1. How does the proposed approach compare to other state-of-the-art methods in the field of safe RL or RL for healthcare? It would be helpful to see a more detailed comparison with existing methods, including their strengths and weaknesses.
2. How does the proposed approach perform on more complex environments that are more representative of real-world healthcare settings? The current experimental evaluation is limited to relatively simple environments, and it would be helpful to see how the approach performs on more complex environments.
3. How sensitive is the proposed approach to different hyperparameters? It would be helpful to see an analysis of the impact of different hyperparameters on the performance of the approach, including their robustness and stability.
4. How does the proposed approach handle different types of uncertainties in healthcare environments? It would be helpful to see an analysis of how the approach handles different types of uncertainties, such as aleatoric uncertainty and epistemic uncertainty.

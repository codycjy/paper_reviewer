# Review

## Summary
This paper studies the relationship between several complexity measures of a learning problem, including its Littlestone dimension, stability, replicability, and differential privacy. The authors show that, for any learning problem, the number of random bits required to achieve a certain level of replicability is tightly bounded by the task's global stability, which is the best replicability that can be achieved by any deterministic algorithm. They further show that the number of random bits required to achieve differential privacy is closely tied to the task's global stability. Finally, they show that a class has bounded randomness complexity (i.e., number of random bits) in the agnostic PAC model if and only if it has finite Littlestone dimension. The results resolve a question about the error-dependent list-replicability of agnostic learning.

## Strengths
The results are interesting, and the authors provide a clear and well-structured presentation of the main ideas and techniques. They also discuss several open problems and future directions. The technical contributions are novel and substantially improve the understanding of randomness complexity in the context of stability and differential privacy. The results are likely to be of interest to the community.

## Weaknesses
The presentation could be improved in some places. For example, it would be helpful to provide a brief explanation of why the constraint in the first part of Theorem 2.2 is reasonable, or at least discuss its implications. The authors might also consider providing a more detailed comparison with the related work of Blonda et al. (2025).

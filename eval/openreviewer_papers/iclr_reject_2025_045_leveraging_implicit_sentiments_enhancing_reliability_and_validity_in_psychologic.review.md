# Review

## Summary
The authors introduce the Core Sentiment Inventory (CSI), a novel psychometric evaluation method designed to assess the emotional tendencies of Large Language Models (LLMs). Inspired by the Implicit Association Test (IAT), the CSI aims to provide a reliable and valid measure of LLMs' implicit sentiment, addressing limitations in current psychometric methods, such as model reluctance and inconsistency.

## Strengths
CSI covers both English and Chinese languages, allowing for a more comprehensive assessment of LLMs' sentiment tendencies, which is valuable given the global reach of these models.

The authors conducted extensive experiments to validate the CSI, demonstrating its effectiveness in quantifying models' sentiments and revealing nuanced emotional patterns.

## Weaknesses
The CSI's reliance on a large set of neutral words may lead to overfitting, especially if the word set is not diverse enough.

The study primarily focuses on GPT-4, GPT-3.5, Qwen2-72B, and Llama3.1-70B models. Including a broader range of models, such as Claude, Gemini, and other open-source options, would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the CSI's applicability and robustness across different architectures and training regimes.

## Questions
What specific measures were taken to ensure that the 5,000 items in the CSI are representative and diverse enough to capture the full range of human-like emotions and psychological traits?

The study focuses on English and Chinese. Have you considered the applicability of the CSI in other languages, and if so, what modifications or challenges might arise?

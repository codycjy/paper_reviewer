# Review

## Summary
The paper studies the expressive power of spectral-invariant GNNs through the lens of homomorphism expressivity. The paper characterizes the class of graphs that can be distinguished by spectral-invariant GNNs and shows that this class is between 1-WL and 2-FWL. The paper also studies the effect of the number of layers on the expressive power, as well as the expressive power of spectral-invariant GNNs over the symmetric powers of the graph.

## Strengths
- The paper is well-written and easy to follow.
- The paper provides a new perspective on the expressive power of spectral-invariant GNNs through the lens of homomorphism expressivity. This perspective allows the paper to establish a hierarchy of GNNs based on their homomorphism expressivity, which leads to new insights into the expressive power of spectral-invariant GNNs.
- The paper also shows that the number of layers in a spectral-invariant GNN affects its expressive power, providing new insights into the role of depth in GNNs.

## Weaknesses
- The paper does not provide any experimental results to support the theoretical findings. It would be helpful to see some empirical evaluation of spectral-invariant GNNs compared to other GNN architectures on real-world datasets.
- The paper focuses on the expressive power of spectral-invariant GNNs, but does not discuss their computational efficiency. It would be helpful to see a discussion on the computational complexity of spectral-invariant GNNs and how it compares to other GNN architectures.
- The paper does not provide any insights into how to design spectral-invariant GNN architectures that balance expressive power and computational efficiency.

## Questions
- How do spectral-invariant GNNs compare to other state-of-the-art GNN architectures in terms of performance on real-world tasks?
- What are the main challenges in designing spectral-invariant GNN architectures that are both highly expressive and computationally efficient?

# Review

## Summary
The paper proposes a new method for score matching with missing data. The authors propose two different methods for missing score matching: an importance weighting approach and a variational approach. They demonstrate the efficacy of their methods on various tasks, including parameter estimation and graphical model estimation.

## Strengths
1. The paper addresses an important problem in the field of score matching, namely, how to handle missing data. The proposed methods are novel and have the potential to improve the performance of score matching in the presence of missing data.
2. The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis of the proposed methods, including finite sample bounds. This analysis helps to understand the behavior of the methods and their performance in different scenarios.
3. The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide clear explanations of the proposed methods and the experimental setup. The figures and tables are also well-designed and help to illustrate the results.

## Weaknesses
1. The paper could benefit from a more extensive experimental evaluation. While the authors demonstrate the efficacy of their methods on various tasks, the experiments are limited to relatively simple scenarios. It would be interesting to see how the methods perform on more complex real-world datasets.
2. The paper could provide more details on the computational complexity of the proposed methods. While the authors briefly discuss the computational cost of the importance weighting approach, a more thorough analysis of the computational complexity of both methods would be helpful.

## Questions
1. Can you provide more details on the computational complexity of your methods, especially the variational approach? How does the computational complexity compare to existing methods for score matching with missing data?
2. How well do the proposed methods perform on more complex real-world datasets? It would be helpful to see experiments on more challenging datasets to better evaluate the efficacy of the methods.
3. How does the performance of the proposed methods depend on the amount of missing data? It would be interesting to see how the methods perform with different levels of missingness.

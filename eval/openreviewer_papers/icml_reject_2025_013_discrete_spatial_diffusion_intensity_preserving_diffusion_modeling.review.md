# Review

## Summary
This paper proposes a discrete spatial diffusion model (DSD) that is designed to preserve discrete intensities exactly during both the forward and reverse diffusion processes. The model accomplishes this by treating each discrete intensity unit as a particle that moves within a spatial lattice according to a stochastic jump process. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of DSD on standard image generation benchmarks (MNIST and CelebA) as well as on scientific data such as rock microstructures and lithium-ion electrode microstructures.

## Strengths
- The paper is well written and easy to follow. The method is well motivated and the theoretical foundation is clearly explained.
- The authors demonstrate DSD on both standard image datasets (MNIST and CelebA) as well as on scientific data (rock and electrode microstructures). The results on the scientific data show promising results for applications in materials science.

## Weaknesses
- While DSD shows promising results for scientific data, its performance on standard image datasets (MNIST and CelebA) appears to be limited. The generated samples have a "blocky" appearance and lack the smoothness and realism of images generated by continuous diffusion models. This suggests that DSD may not be suitable for general-purpose image generation. It would be helpful if the authors could provide a more detailed analysis of the limitations of DSD for image generation.
- The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with existing discrete diffusion models. While the authors mention some related work, they do not provide a detailed comparison of DSD with other discrete diffusion models on standard image generation tasks. Such a comparison would help to better position DSD within the broader context of discrete diffusion models and highlight its unique strengths and weaknesses.
- The authors mention that DSD introduces a steeper learning curve compared to standard Gaussian diffusion models. It would be helpful to provide a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of this learning curve, such as the amount of time or expertise required to implement DSD effectively.

## Questions
- How does DSD compare to other discrete diffusion models on standard image generation tasks? A more comprehensive comparison with existing discrete diffusion models would help to better position DSD within the broader context of discrete diffusion models and highlight its unique strengths and weaknesses.
- What are the practical implications of the steeper learning curve introduced by DSD? How much time or expertise is required to implement DSD effectively? A more detailed discussion of the practical implications would be helpful for potential users of DSD.
- How well does DSD scale to higher-resolution images or datasets with higher intensity saturation? The authors mention that DSD becomes less efficient than other techniques for higher-resolution images or datasets with higher intensity saturation. Can DSD still preserve intensity exactly in these cases, or are there limitations to its performance at higher resolutions?
- How does DSD perform on datasets with a wider range of intensity values? The authors mention that DSD is particularly well-suited for datasets with low-bit depth or binary data. How does DSD perform on datasets with a wider range of intensity values, such as standard uint8 images? Are there limitations to its performance on these datasets?

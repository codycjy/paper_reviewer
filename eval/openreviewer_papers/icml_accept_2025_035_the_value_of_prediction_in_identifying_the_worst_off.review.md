# Review

## Summary
This paper considers the problem of using machine learning to identify the worst-off members of a population, with the goal of prioritizing them for further screening and support. In particular, the authors study the problem of how to balance the cost of expanding screening capacity with the cost of improving the accuracy of the predictive model. The authors provide a series of theoretical results, and complement them with a case study on identifying long-term unemployment.

## Strengths
The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The theoretical results are interesting and complemented with a compelling case study. The paper also provides some guidance for policymakers on how to think about the tradeoffs between expanding screening capacity and improving the accuracy of the predictive model.

## Weaknesses
It is not clear how the theoretical results would change if the goal was to balance the costs of expanding screening capacity with the costs of keeping and training existing screeners. In this case, the costs of screening would be roughly linear in the size of the population being screened, rather than fixed. It would be interesting to see how this would change the theoretical results, and whether or not the results from the case study would change as well.

## Questions
- How would the theoretical results change if the costs of screening were roughly linear in the size of the population being screened (as opposed to fixed)?
- How would the results from the case study change if the costs of screening were roughly linear in the size of the population being screened (as opposed to fixed)?

# Review

## Summary
This paper introduces Knowledge graph Assisted Reasoning Path Aggregation (KARPA), a framework that leverages the global planning capabilities of LLMs for KGQA. The method operates through three steps: pre-planning, retrieving, and reasoning. It generates relation paths using the LLM's planning abilities, retrieves candidate paths using an embedding model, and then reasons over these paths to answer questions. Extensive experiments on WebQSP and CWQ datasets demonstrate that KARPA achieves state-of-the-art performance.

## Strengths
1. The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide clear explanations of the KARPA framework and its components.
2. The proposed method is straightforward and effective, achieving strong performance on two benchmark datasets.

## Weaknesses
1. The novelty of the proposed method is limited. The approach of using an LLM to generate candidate paths and then retrieving similar paths from a knowledge graph has been previously explored in methods like RoG and ToG. The main difference in KARPA is the introduction of a semantic embedding model to identify candidate relations, which is a relatively minor innovation.
2. The paper lacks an analysis of the quality and diversity of the relation paths generated by the LLM during the pre-planning phase. Understanding the characteristics of these paths is crucial for the performance of the retrieval and reasoning steps. An analysis could provide insights into the effectiveness of the pre-planning process and help identify potential improvements.
3. The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational efficiency and resource requirements of KARPA. Given the potential overhead of using LLMs and semantic embedding models in a three-step framework, it would be beneficial to compare the performance gains against the increased computational cost. This analysis would help evaluate the practical applicability of KARPA in real-world scenarios.

## Questions
1. How does the performance of KARPA change when dealing with knowledge graphs in languages other than English? What modifications, if any, are needed to the framework to support multilingual contexts?
2. How does KARPA handle questions that require commonsense reasoning or world knowledge not explicitly present in the knowledge graph? Is there a mechanism to fallback to LLM's internal knowledge when no matching reasoning paths are found in the KG?
